# Meeting with Allen Chafa (Thursday, May 18th)

## Current Situation
- Once a single sensor failed, the whole system would collapse.
- Underestimated the damages caused to sensors by minerals.
- Biggest challenge: Technical skills, team is scattered.
- Most systems are chlorine dosing system (without monitoring).
- UV water purification systems.
- People are realising borehole water is not safe. Certain boreholes may be closed.
- Chlorine tablets are fairly accessible, but not desirable (taste, smell).
- Many boreholes. Community boreholes (managed by local councils). There are also households or neighborhood groups that make their own borehole.
- Fuzzy logic worked well in theory. However, when one or two sensors failed, the whole system would collapse. There shouuld be better approaches. TinyML might be an option.
- No license required for chlorination of water.
- Physical and chemical parameters measured. We can extrapolate levels of biological concentration. Need to measure chlorine residual.
- The six he chose followed the WHO guidelines. Some of them were not necessary, but could be important for chlorine residual measurements.
- Sensor Degradation: Nutrient-dense water requires sensors to be frequently re-calibrated. Sensors not broken, but need technical people to reset them. 

## Recommended Next Steps: 
- Work on what we are interested.
- Clean slate.
- Is it possible to develop a system that can monitor and then adapt to various purification systems?
- Price: Not a focus right now. Need a working product to evaluate WTP. Currently, people pay 20 GBP a month for drinking water. Subscription model (40-60 users per borehole). Each household pays less than 20 GBP per month for access. Community-based approach is cheaper. 

## Zimbabwe: 
- Maintenance: People lack the technical skills for repairs in households.
- Need sensors to be readily available (he was sourcing them from China).
- Many boreholes (community and household level).
